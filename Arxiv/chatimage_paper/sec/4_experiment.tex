We evaluate \method{} along two axes: \textbf{image quality} (does the
generated visual answer faithfully and clearly convey the structured content?)
and \textbf{visual-alignment accuracy} (do the clickable hotspots land on the
correct regions of the generated image?). We assemble a benchmark spanning
multiple content types and conduct a human evaluation, since both image
quality and region alignment are inherently perceptual and not captured by a
single automatic metric. Quantitative values are reported as mean$\pm$std
across annotators (with per-question scores averaged before aggregation);
entries marked \tbd{XX} are pending completion of the full evaluation and
are left as placeholders in this version.

We assemble a benchmark of 30 questions designed to exercise different
visual modes and content types, summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:benchmark_split}. The current public showcase contains selected
outputs from the same \method{} workflow after strict visual-alignment
screening: a hand-drawn West Lake tour map and daily-life illustrated scenes
such as a smart-home living room, boutique coffee shop, sunny reading nook,
independent record-store corner, and indoor plant-care corner. Map questions
test geographic region placement and route-like scenic areas, while scene
questions test object localization in less rigidly structured images. The
broader benchmark also includes infographic-style planning and
decision-support prompts to measure how compact analytical regions behave,
but only runs that pass the current strict grounding and mask-quality gate are
promoted to the public demo page. Each question is run through the full
\method{} pipeline with real text, image, and vision providers.
Because the grounding step is provider-agnostic
(Section~\ref{sec:method_alignment}), individual benchmark items may be
served by different grounding backends; we report the per-module alignment
source distribution alongside the aggregate scores so the contribution of
each backend (LocateAnything-3B, MiMo-Vision, SAM3-refined planned bounds,
and planned-fallback) is visible. Representative qualitative results are
shown in Figure~\ref{fig:qualitative}.

\paragraph{Manual alignment audit.}
Before the human evaluation, each of the 30 questions is run once through the
full pipeline and screened by the automatic strict-alignment gate
(Section~\ref{sec:method_alignment}), which requires every hotspot to carry a
real grounding primary source (LocateAnything-3B or MiMo-Vision) and a SAM3
mask with a valid organic preview. Of the 30 runs, 15 completed the full
pipeline (basic success rate $50.0\%$; the remaining 15 failed at the
text-to-image step due to upstream API rate-limiting). Since the automatic
gate can over-count plausible but visually loose matches, we use a manual
audit of visible hotspot alignments as the reported grounding figure. In this
audit, \textbf{17 of 24 inspected hotspots} are judged correctly aligned,
yielding a conditional manual alignment success rate of \textbf{$70.8\%$}. The human
evaluation below is conducted on the completed runs, so the IQ/AA scores
reflect cases where the pipeline produced an image.

\subsection{Human Evaluation}
\label{sec:experiments_human}

Because image quality and region alignment are perceptual, we conducted a human
evaluation. Annotators were shown the generated interactive visual image (with
hotspots) alongside the original question and raw answer, and rated two
dimensions on a 1--5 Likert scale:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
    \item \textbf{Image quality (IQ):} Does the image faithfully and clearly
    convey the structured content of the answer? (1=incoherent, 5=clear and
    faithful.)
    \item \textbf{Alignment accuracy (AA):} When clicking each hotspot, does
    the opened detail correspond to the visual region under the cursor?
    (1=completely misaligned, 5=perfectly aligned.)
\end{itemize}

Each question was evaluated by \tbd{K} annotators; we report the mean score and
inter-annotator agreement (Krippendorff's $\alpha$).
Table~\ref{tab:main_results} reports the overall scores and per-mode breakdown.
As baselines, we compare against (i)~a \textbf{planned-only} variant that uses
planned layout bounds without vision alignment (simulating the common
assumption that the generator follows the layout), and (ii)~a \textbf{text-only}
condition where the annotator receives only the raw text answer and rates its
navigability on the same scale, to contextualize the value of the visual
interactive format.

\subsection{Alignment Ablation}
\label{sec:experiments_ablation}

To isolate the contribution of the two-pass vision alignment, we ablated the
grounding source while keeping the rest of the pipeline fixed.
Table~\ref{tab:ablation_results} compares: (a)~planned bounds only (no
grounding), (b)~LocateAnything grounding without SAM3 refinement, (c)~full
pipeline with SAM3 mask refinement, and (d)~the robustness design (keeping
far-from-planned groundings, falling back only for header/cross-panel rejects
and missing modules). We expected grounding to substantially improve alignment
accuracy over planned-only bounds, since the latter assume layout fidelity that
generative models do not provide. The robustness ablation tests whether
preserving correctly-grounded modules when some fail (rather than discarding
all alignments) improves overall alignment.

Table~\ref{tab:task_breakdown} breaks down image quality and alignment
accuracy by visual mode. We expect alignment to be easiest in infographic
mode, where regions are grid/flow-structured and boundaries are clear, and
hardest in scene mode, where objects are irregularly placed and grounding must
distinguish overlapping elements. Map mode presents an intermediate case with
route and landmark localization. This breakdown helps identify which content
types the current grounding pipeline handles well and where future work
(polygons, finer masks) is most needed.
