We evaluate \method{} along two axes: \textbf{image quality} (does the
generated visual answer faithfully and clearly convey the structured content?)
and \textbf{visual-alignment accuracy} (do the clickable hotspots land on the
correct regions of the generated image?). We assemble a benchmark spanning
multiple content types and conduct a human evaluation, since both image
quality and region alignment are inherently perceptual and not captured by a
single automatic metric. Quantitative values are reported as mean$\pm$std over
multiple runs; entries marked \tbd{XX} are pending completion of the full
evaluation and are left as placeholders in this version.

We assemble a benchmark of \tbd{N} questions designed to exercise different
visual modes and content types, summarized in
Table~\ref{tab:benchmark_split}. Infographic questions cover technical
processes and comparisons (e.g., ``compare REST and GraphQL,'' ``explain the
RAG retrieval pipeline,'' ``draw the OAuth\,2.0 authorization-code flow''),
which test structured flow and layered layouts. Map questions cover spatial
guidance (e.g., ``draw a hand-drawn guide map of West Lake''), testing
geographic region placement and route hotspots. Scene questions cover
illustrated layouts (e.g., ``illustrate a museum scene''), testing object
localization in a less rigidly structured image. Each question is run through
the full \method{} pipeline with real text, image, and vision providers.

\subsection{Human Evaluation}
\label{sec:experiments_human}

Because image quality and region alignment are perceptual, we conduct a human
evaluation. Annotators are shown the generated interactive visual image (with
hotspots) alongside the original question and raw answer, and rate two
dimensions on a 1--5 Likert scale:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
    \item \textbf{Image quality (IQ):} Does the image faithfully and clearly
    convey the structured content of the answer? (1=incoherent, 5=clear and
    faithful.)
    \item \textbf{Alignment accuracy (AA):} When clicking each hotspot, does
    the opened detail correspond to the visual region under the cursor?
    (1=completely misaligned, 5=perfectly aligned.)
\end{itemize}

Each question is evaluated by \tbd{K} annotators; we report the mean score and
inter-annotator agreement (Krippendorff's $\alpha$).
Table~\ref{tab:main_results} reports the overall scores and per-mode breakdown.
As baselines, we compare against (i)~a \textbf{planned-only} variant that uses
planned layout bounds without vision alignment (simulating the common
assumption that the generator follows the layout), and (ii)~a \textbf{text-only}
condition where the annotator receives only the raw text answer and rates its
navigability on the same scale, to contextualize the value of the visual
interactive format.

\subsection{Alignment Ablation}
\label{sec:experiments_ablation}

To isolate the contribution of the two-pass vision alignment, we ablate the
grounding source while keeping the rest of the pipeline fixed.
Table~\ref{tab:ablation_results} compares: (a)~planned bounds only (no
grounding), (b)~LocateAnything grounding without SAM3 refinement, (c)~full
pipeline with SAM3 mask refinement, and (d)~the robustness design (keeping
far-from-planned groundings, falling back only for header/cross-panel rejects
and missing modules). We expect grounding to substantially improve alignment
accuracy over planned-only bounds, since the latter assume layout fidelity that
generative models do not provide. The robustness ablation tests whether
preserving correctly-grounded modules when some fail (rather than discarding
all alignments) improves overall alignment.

\label{sec:experiments_permode}

\input{table/task_breakdown}

Table~\ref{tab:task_breakdown} breaks down image quality and alignment
accuracy by visual mode. We expect alignment to be easiest in infographic
mode, where regions are grid/flow-structured and boundaries are clear, and
hardest in scene mode, where objects are irregularly placed and grounding must
distinguish overlapping elements. Map mode presents an intermediate case with
route and landmark localization. This breakdown helps identify which content
types the current grounding pipeline handles well and where future work
(polygons, finer masks) is most needed.
